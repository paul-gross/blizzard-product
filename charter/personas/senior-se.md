# `persona:senior-se` — the senior software engineer

## Who they are

They can absolutely code — that has never been the question. What they are not, yet, is an orchestrator of fleets. They don't reliably know in advance what the finished code should look like or how the solution ought to be architected, they may struggle to contribute to up-front planning, and — most importantly — they may not trust what a model produces. They have not yet made the leap from reviewing every step a machine takes to supervising the outcomes it delivers: from human-in-the-loop to human-on-the-loop.

That hesitation deserves respect, not correction. Some of it is earned experience.

## What their work looks like

They want to hand work to the fleet the way they would hand it to a new team member: small pieces first, everything checked, responsibility extended as it is earned. A hand-off that assumes deep harness fluency — an archaeology project to find out what an agent was doing — would end the experiment on day one.

## What they need from blizzard

The fleet's structure substitutes for orchestration skill they haven't built yet. Work arrives already shaped, so deciding *how* something gets executed is not their burden. Output arrives in trustable units — per-task verdicts, small blast radius. And when the fleet needs them, the escalation is a pasteable command and the agent's full session context, not a puzzle.

Above all, they need the dial. They start with a human gate at every station — in the workflow graph, or imposed by their own runner on any node by name — and remove gates one at a time as confidence grows. Blizzard is their on-ramp from HITL to HOTL, and it builds their confidence the only way that lasts: by being structurally unable to lie about status or slip work past a gate they configured. Trust is earned gate by gate, never demanded as an entry fee.

## What serves them

Fail-safe verdicts from the supervisor, the configurable human gates, the planner's routing doctrine, and the board.
